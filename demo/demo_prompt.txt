France, officially the French Republic, is a country located primarily in Western Europe, with several overseas regions and territories spread across other continents. Its metropolitan territory extends from the Rhine to the Atlantic Ocean and from the Mediterranean Sea to the English Channel and the North Sea. It shares land borders with Belgium, Luxembourg, Germany, Switzerland, Italy, Spain, Andorra, and Monaco, while its overseas territories give it frontiers with Brazil and Suriname in South America. France is the largest country in the European Union by area, and its capital, Paris, has long been a global centre for art, fashion, gastronomy, philosophy, and science, and it remains one of the most visited cities in the world.

The history of France stretches back through the Roman conquest of Gaul under Julius Caesar, the gradual settlement of Germanic tribes, and the rise of the medieval kingdom of the Franks under rulers such as Clovis and Charlemagne, whose empire briefly united much of Western Europe. The Middle Ages saw the slow consolidation of royal authority, the construction of great Gothic cathedrals at Chartres, Reims, and Amiens, and the long rivalry with England that culminated in the Hundred Years War, during which Joan of Arc became a national symbol. The Renaissance brought new currents of humanism and learning, while the seventeenth century, often called the grand century, witnessed the absolute monarchy of Louis the Fourteenth, the splendour of the palace of Versailles, and the flowering of classical French literature in the works of Moliere, Racine, and Corneille.

The Revolution of 1789 overturned the old order, abolished feudal privileges, and proclaimed the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, ideals that would echo across the world. The turbulent decade that followed gave rise to the Republic, the Terror, and ultimately to Napoleon Bonaparte, whose conquests and legal reforms, especially the Napoleonic Code, reshaped the institutions of much of continental Europe. The nineteenth century brought alternating monarchies, empires, and republics, rapid industrialisation, the expansion of a vast colonial empire across Africa and Asia, and the cultural ferment of Romanticism, Realism, and Impressionism in painting and letters.

The twentieth century was marked by the immense sacrifices of the First World War, the occupation and liberation of the Second, and the subsequent loss of empire through the wars of decolonisation in Indochina and Algeria. In the decades after 1945, France rebuilt its economy, helped to found the institutions that would become the European Union, and developed an independent nuclear deterrent. Today France is a founding member of the European Union and the United Nations, a permanent member of the Security Council, a member of the Group of Seven, and one of the largest economies in the world, with strengths in aerospace, automobiles, luxury goods, energy, agriculture, and tourism.

The French state is a unitary semi-presidential republic, governed under the Constitution of the Fifth Republic, which was established in 1958 under the leadership of Charles de Gaulle. Executive power is shared between a President, elected directly by the people for a five-year term, and a Prime Minister responsible to the National Assembly. The country is divided into eighteen administrative regions, thirteen of them in metropolitan Europe and five overseas, which are further subdivided into departments, arrondissements, cantons, and communes, the smallest unit of local government and one of the oldest in the nation.

French culture continues to exert a profound influence far beyond the borders of the country. Its language, spoken across five continents and serving as an official language of dozens of nations and international organisations, remains among the most widely studied on Earth. French cuisine, with its regional traditions and its emphasis on technique and the quality of ingredients, has been recognised as part of the intangible heritage of humanity. The nation's contributions to mathematics, physics, chemistry, medicine, and the cinema, from the pioneering films of the Lumiere brothers to the present day, testify to a long and continuing engagement with both the sciences and the arts. From the vineyards of Bordeaux and Burgundy to the lavender fields of Provence, from the beaches of Normandy to the peaks of the Alps and the Pyrenees, the landscapes of France are as varied as its history is long.
